A bivariate VAR(1) lets two series feed back on each other: y₁ₜ = a₁₁ y₁,t−1 + a₁₂ y₂,t−1 + e₁ₜ and y₂ₜ = a₂₁ y₁,t−1 + a₂₂ y₂,t−1 + e₂ₜ. The off-diagonal terms carry the cross-effects: a₁₂ is whether y₂ helps predict y₁ (Granger causality), and the impulse responses show how a one-off shock propagates over time. Shocks here are orthogonal and of unit size, so the response at horizon h is simply Ah.

Why this matters — real examples

The VAR is the workhorse of empirical macro: the standard way to ask "if we shock this, what happens to everything else, and for how long?" The impulse responses you trace here are the exact object used in policy analysis.

Monetary policy"What does a rate rise actually do?" is an impulse response

The central question of empirical macro, tracing a surprise interest-rate hike through inflation, output and unemployment over the following quarters, is literally an IRF from a VAR. Sims won the 2011 Nobel for putting VARs at the centre of this. Shock one variable above and read the response: that is the exercise.

→ The link: every "effect of monetary policy" chart you've seen in a lecture is a VAR impulse response.

Monetary policyThe famous "price puzzle"

Early VARs found that a rate hike seemed to briefly raise inflation, the "price puzzle", which sent macroeconomists back to fix their identifying assumptions. It's a great cautionary tale: an IRF is only as credible as the ordering and identification you impose on the shocks.

→ The link: the widget uses a fixed orthogonal ordering; in real work, choosing that ordering is the hard, contested part.

MacroGranger causality: does X help forecast Y?

Does money growth Granger-cause inflation? Do oil prices Granger-cause recessions? "Granger causality" just asks whether lags of X improve the forecast of Y, i.e. testing whether the off-diagonal coefficient is zero. It's a statement about prediction, not true causation, a distinction that trips up almost everyone at first.

→ The link: the Granger readouts here are exactly the a₁₂ = 0 / a₂₁ = 0 tests, phrased as "does it help predict?"

Housing & ratesFeedback a single equation would miss

Interest rates and house prices feed back on each other, as do wages and prices. A one-equation model treats one side as exogenous and misses the loop entirely. A VAR lets both respond to each other, which is why policy institutions forecast with systems rather than single equations.

→ The link: the two off-diagonal coefficients are the feedback channel; set them to zero and the system decouples into two unrelated ARs.

How to use it in class

Set a₁₂ = 0 and the "shock to y₂" panel shows y₁ flat, y₂ does not Granger-cause y₁. Give it a value and y₁ now responds. Push the diagonal terms up toward 1 and the responses decay more slowly (more persistent system); cross the stationarity boundary and the badge flips and responses stop dying out. This is the IRF and Granger-causality content of question 6.
