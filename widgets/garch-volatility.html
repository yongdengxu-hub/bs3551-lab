GARCH volatility

Volatility clustering, persistence and Value-at-Risk. Exam question 3.

← back to lab

Financial returns are not equally volatile through time: calm spells and turbulent spells cluster. A GARCH(1,1) captures this by letting today's variance depend on yesterday's shock and yesterday's variance, σ²t = ω + α r²t−1 + β σ²t−1. The sum α+β is the persistence: push it toward 1 and the clustering becomes long-lived. Switch to GJR to add a leverage term so bad news raises volatility more than good news.

Why this matters — real examples

Volatility clustering is the single most robust fact about asset returns, and a whole industry is built on forecasting it. Almost everything here is finance, because that is where GARCH earns its keep.

Financial markets2008 and COVID look identical in one respect

In the 2008 crisis and again in March 2020, daily equity volatility didn't just jump, it stayed high for weeks, then slowly calmed. A constant-variance model can't do that; GARCH can, because a big shock feeds a big variance which feeds the next. Set α+β near 0.99 above to reproduce that "calm, then a storm that lingers".

→ The link: the persistence α+β you tune is literally how long a burst of turbulence hangs around.

Financial marketsThe VIX "fear index" is a volatility forecast

The VIX you see on the news is essentially the market's forecast of near-term S&P 500 volatility, the same object σ²t+1 this widget produces. When it spikes toward 80 (2008, 2020), that is the conditional variance blowing up. You are building a toy VIX.

→ The link: the variance-forecast panel on the right is the same quantity a volatility index reports.

RegulationBank capital under Basel is VaR from a volatility model

Banks must hold capital against a 1-day (and 10-day) Value at Risk, and VaR = z × σt. A better volatility forecast literally changes how much capital a bank sets aside. The 1-day VaR metric in this widget is the exact quantity the Basel rules are built on.

→ The link: get σt wrong and the bank is either under-capitalised or leaving money idle.

Financial marketsLeverage: bad news hurts more than good news

After Lehman collapsed, volatility rose far more than it did after equally large up days. That asymmetry is the leverage effect, and symmetric GARCH misses it, which is why you switch on the GJR γ term. Turn γ up and watch a negative shock kick volatility higher than a positive one of the same size.

→ The link: the GJR toggle is exactly the fix real risk desks use (GJR / EGARCH) to price this asymmetry.

How to use it in class

Start with persistence near 0.95 and point out the breathing band, that is volatility clustering. Drag α+β down toward 0.5 and the clustering disappears (returns look like constant-variance noise). Switch to GJR and show the leverage term lifting volatility after negative returns. The forecast panel makes the mean-reversion of variance to ω/(1−α−β) explicit, which is the multi-step forecast on the exam.
