Regime switching: TAR / STAR

A sharp threshold versus a smooth logistic transition. Exam question 4.

← back to lab

Many series behave differently in different states: expansion vs recession, low vs high inflation. A two-regime model runs one AR(1) when the transition variable is low and another when it is high. TAR flips between them sharply at a threshold; STAR uses a logistic function to move smoothly from one regime to the other, with γ controlling how abrupt the switch is. The transition curve on the left is the heart of it: as γ grows, the smooth STAR curve approaches the TAR step.

Why this matters — real examples

The economy is not one machine with fixed gears; it behaves differently in booms and busts, in calm and crisis. A single linear AR averages those states into one wrong model. Regime switching is how we stop doing that.

Monetary policyCentral banks react nonlinearly to inflation

A central bank tolerates inflation drifting inside a comfort zone, then reacts sharply once it breaches a threshold: a TAR-style response, not a smooth linear one. The 2022–23 tightening looked exactly like a regime flip, dovish and then abruptly aggressive once inflation cleared roughly 4–5%.

↳ The link: one AR line can't capture "relaxed below the threshold, aggressive above it"; a two-regime model can.

Business cycleExpansions and recessions are different worlds

Hamilton's regime-switching model, part of the work behind his 2022 Nobel share, formalised what everyone sensed: growth in a recession has different dynamics (deeper, faster) than in an expansion. The NBER and UK recession datings are essentially regime labels stamped on the data.

↳ The link: fit a linear AR across a full cycle and you get a model that describes neither regime well.

LabourUnemployment rises fast but falls slowly

This asymmetry is a textbook regime nonlinearity: the job-loss regime and the job-finding regime move at different speeds, so unemployment rockets up in a downturn and drifts down only gradually in a recovery. STAR's smooth transition captures that gradual change of gear.

↳ The link: the smoothness parameter γ you control is exactly "how gradually the economy shifts regime".

Local housingBooms and busts are not mirror images

House prices in a boom (momentum, rapid rises) behave differently from a bust, where declines are sticky because sellers refuse to cut. That is a regime switch on the price-change threshold, and it is why linear house-price models fit so badly across a full cycle.

↳ The link: the sharp-vs-smooth choice (TAR vs STAR) is really a question about how suddenly a housing market turns.

How to use it in class

On STAR, slide γ from small to large and watch the transition curve sharpen from a gentle S-shape into the TAR step, that is the single picture that explains "smooth" transition. Set very different persistences (φ₁ low, φ₂ near 1) so the two regimes visibly behave differently, then switch to TAR to contrast the hard boundary. The colouring of the series shows which regime each observation sits in.
