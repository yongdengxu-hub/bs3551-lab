Every stationary process leaves a signature in its autocorrelation (ACF) and partial autocorrelation (PACF). Why this matters — real examples

Identifying whether a series is a random walk or a stationary ARMA is the first move in any applied project. Here is where that decision bites, in the areas you care about.

Financial marketsStock prices vs returns, and the efficient-market hypothesis

The FTSE 100 level behaves like a random walk: its ACF sits near 1 and dies away only very slowly, so today's price is your best guess of tomorrow's. Daily returns, though, look like white noise, with almost every ACF bar inside the band. That flat ACF is the empirical face of the efficient-market hypothesis.

↳ The link: if past returns showed a big ACF spike, momentum traders could exploit it. Mostly they can't, which is exactly why the return ACF is flat.

Monetary policyHow sticky is UK inflation?

CPI inflation has an ACF that tails off gradually: a persistent AR process. The Bank of England watches that persistence closely, because a highly persistent series means an energy shock (2022) takes years, not months, to fade, which is part of why Bank Rate stayed high well into 2024.

↳ The link: the decay rate in the ACF is essentially how long inflation "remembers" a shock.

Local housingA Cardiff house-price index needs differencing

A local price index trends upward for years: classic non-stationarity, ACF near 1. You can't fit an ARMA to it directly. You difference it into price growth, which is stationary and forecastable. "Is it I(1)?" is the question every applied housing paper opens with.

↳ The link: an ACF that refuses to die tells you to difference first, then model.

LabourWhy unemployment scars last

The UK unemployment rate is highly persistent (near a unit root): its ACF barely decays over a year. That persistence is the statistical signature of "hysteresis", the idea that recessions leave long-lasting damage, while the PACF cutting off at lag 1 or 2 tells you a low-order AR captures most of the dynamics.

↳ The link: persistence you can read straight off the ACF is what makes labour-market shocks so hard to shake.

How to use it in class

Project the quiz, give students 20 seconds per plot, take a show of hands before revealing. Or set it as a 10-minute warm-up before the ARMA tutorial. The "show the maths" panel links each picture to the Yule–Walker / moment derivations they need for the written exam.
